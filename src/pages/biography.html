OSKAR
SCHLEMMER

GERMAN BAUHAUS PAINTER, SCULPTOR, DESIGNER AND CHOREOGRAPHER

Source: Oskar Schlemmer's Self Portrait with Mask (1923)
01

THE ORIGIN

Born in the city of Stuttgart in 1888, Schlemmer’s journey began with an apprenticeship in an inlay workshop from 1903 to 1905. Throughout his apprenticeship, he studied at the Kunstgewerbeschule and later earned a scholarship to attend the Kunstakademie. In 1910, Schlemmer moved to Berlin, where he produced some of his finest works before moving back to Stuttgart to become the master pupil of Adolf Hölzel.

Schlemmer was enlisted on the Western Front in World War I in 1914 but faced many wounds. He returned to the Kunstakademie and began experimenting with sculptures. He exhibited his work at Der Sturm gallery in Berlin. During the year he married Helena Tutein, Schlemmer received an invitation from Walter Gropius to teach in Weimar.

02

THE BAUHAUS ERA

Schlemmer initially took control over the mural-painting and sculpture departments at the Bauhaus school. He had made significant contributions to each department offered in the school, yet left his most influential mark on the theatre with the creation of the Triadisches Ballett.

During his period serving as head of the stage workshop in Weimar and Dessau, Schlemmer's work involved exploring the human body and space. The influence of his ballet inspired a series of paintings, featuring faceless, stylized figures, predominantly of women. His fascination with the subject eventually culminated in a course he taught titled "Der Mensch." Regrettably, Schlemmer left the Bauhaus in 1929, largely due to the increasingly political climate occurring in Germany.

03

THE LEGACY

Schlemmer relocated to Breslau, where he continued teaching and painted his most renowned piece Bauhaus Stairway. He accepted a professorship at the Berlin Vereinigte Staatsschulen für freie und angewandte Kunst until he was dismissed by the Nazi regime in 1933. His work fell victim to the oppressive regime, where most have been destroyed or exhibited in the 1937 “Degenerate Art” exhibition in Munich.

Schlemmer spent his final years in "inner emigration," working at the Institut für Malstoffe in Wuppertal, where he was allowed to paint without further persecution. He created his final series of 18 works titled "Fensterbilder" before tragically passing in a heart attack in 1943.
